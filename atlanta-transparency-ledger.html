What this is

Citizens own the surveillance data. Citizens watch the people who use it.

Every camera lookup, every plate scan, every footage retrieval is written to a public ledger. Citizens enroll as co-owners and earn dividends in US Treasury-backed stablecoins. Citizens deploy AI agents that flag bad-faith reports, missed follow-ups, and false statements. Every government access request requires a public vote. The data stays private with the residents who own it.

This is a public dashboard for the cameras in your neighborhood. Every time someone looks at the footage, you see it here. Every search, every requestor, every reason. The cameras stay where they are. What changes is who controls the data and who answers when somebody uses it.

Universal framework. Atlanta is the flagship. Any city, any country, any nationality of resident can adopt it. Witnesses, reporters, undercover officers, and ordinary residents are all protected by the same identity-shielding rules. The data shown here is illustrative. The framework is real and ready to deploy.

Where things stand today

The current contract, in plain language
Right now

Atlanta runs about 250 license-plate cameras under a $3.8M contract with Flock Safety.

The Atlanta Police Department contracts Flock Safety for the city's automated license-plate reader network. The "Connect Atlanta" initiative extends that network by integrating private camera feeds donated by businesses, HOAs, and residents.

The cameras themselves are not the issue. The issue is who controls the data they collect: today, that's a private vendor with employee access logs, federal sharing pathways, no community oversight, and no audit log accessible to the people being recorded.

Architectures this framework refuses

Vendors rebrand. Categories endure.
The renewal contract must reject these architectures regardless of which vendor offers them.
  • ×Centralized biometric identification. Iris, face, fingerprint, vein, or gait binding to digital identity. Biometrics cannot be rotated like a password.
  • ×Unified identity profile architectures. Combining digital ID, wallets, social handles, and demographic markers in one verifiable profile is the substrate of a social credit system.
  • ×AI-tethered verification. Coupling AI tool access to biometric or unified-identity verification converts a productivity tool into a coercion architecture.
  • ×Surveillance-as-monetization business models. Selling, licensing, or advertising-against citizen movement or behavioral data, even when packaged as “anonymized.”
  • ×Cross-jurisdictional individual tracking via public-records joins. Products built to track specific individuals across state lines without judicial authorization.
  • ×Multi-agency databases without local audit. Plate or biometric databases shared across thousands of agencies with no audit log accessible to the source jurisdiction.

Economic Sovereignty

The data dividend belongs to the public. Paid in dollars.

Movement, traffic, and pattern-of-life analytics carry real commercial value. Under the legacy model, that value flows to vendors as licensing revenue. Under this framework, it returns to residents as a community dividend.

Dividends are paid in US Treasury-backed stablecoins. Each token is fully reserved one-to-one by short-term US Treasury bills, repurchase agreements, and segregated cash held with regulated US custodians. Tokens are redeemable on demand for US dollars and function as dollar-equivalent payment instruments under emerging federal stablecoin frameworks. The cooperative is framework-agnostic on which regulated issuer it uses, and the choice is subject to community vote.

This is not anti-enforcement. It is citizen ownership of citizen-funded infrastructure, paid out in legal dollar-equivalent currency.
